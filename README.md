**1. Executive Summary**
Bearledict Computebatch is an autonomous and interactive robot guide designed to help students and visitors navigate the second floor of UCSD's CSE building. Deployed near the existing floor map, the robot allows users to ask natural language questions about nearby offices, rooms, restrooms, and lounges, then physically guides them to their destination while sharing CSE-related fun facts along the way. The system integrates a Turtlebot, ROS-based navigation using AprilTag localization, a speech and LLM query pipeline for intent recognition, and an expressive voice and visual persona modeled after English actor Benedict Cumberbatch. A user study with four participants found that interacting with Bearledict resulted in high ratings for friendliness, personality, and reduced navigation stress, with all participants confirming the robot correctly identified their requested destination, while also identifying areas for improvement in speech recognition and movement predictability.

**2. Introduction**
Bearledict Computebatch (Bearledict) is a robot that resides in the CSE building (specifically on the second-floor for the scope of our project) and acts as an interactive guide designed to help students find professors’ offices, rooms, lounges, restrooms, and more in the UCSD CSE building. The robot will be stationed near the existing second-floor map and will act as an embodied, interactive version of it. Instead of requiring students to interpret a static floor layout or mindlessly wander through the maze of the CSE’ Building’s many halls on their own, the robot will allow them to ask natural language questions such as “Where is Professor Riek’s office?” or “How do I get to room 2218?” The robot will then identify the requested destination, and guide the student through the hallway, while occasionally sharing CSE-related fun facts or jokes with the user. The motivation for this project comes from a common student experience: trying to find a professor’s office or conference room in an unfamiliar academic building with endless hallways and hidden rooms, which can be incredibly confusing and stressful. This can even go as far as to inhibit students from attending office hours, asking for help, or meeting a professor for the first time. Our goal is to reduce this stress by creating a robot that provides clear directions in a friendly and low-pressure manner. By making the existing CSE map interactive, mobile, and conversational, we hope to make the second floor easier to navigate and more welcoming for UCSD students. 

Our project is inspired by several prior HRI and mobile robotics systems. RHINO, an interactive museum tour-guide robot, demonstrated how an autonomous robot could guide visitors through a public environment while combining navigation, localization, and user interaction. This work is closely related to our goal because our robot also acts as a guide in a public indoor space (specifically, a leader role for loose association groups), though our setting is an academic building rather than a museum (Burgard et al., 1998). Our project also connects strongly to office-guide robot research. Pacchierotti, Christensen, and Jensfelt designed an office-guide robot that helps visitors locate offices while also studying how robots should behave during hallway encounters. Their work is especially relevant because our robot’s primary task is also to help users find offices in a building with shared corridors (Pacchierotti et al., 2006). In addition, research on human-aware robot navigation emphasizes that robots should not only avoid collisions, but also move in ways that respect human comfort, social spacing, and legibility. Kruse et al.’s survey frames navigation around people as a human-aware design problem, which supports our plan to evaluate whether the robot feels safe and comfortable in the CSE hallway environment (Kruse et al., 2013). Mavrogiannis et al.’s survey on social robot navigation similarly highlights that robot navigation in crowded public spaces requires attention to planning, behavior design, and evaluation methodology. This motivates our focus on both technical navigation accuracy and the user’s experience of the robot’s movement (Mavrogiannis et al., 2023).

Drawing on these ideas, we developed Bearledict to explore how an interactive robot guide could improve navigation with the CSE Building. To evaluate Bearledict, we designed a user study aimed at answering the following research questions:
Can an embodied robot make a confusing static map easier to use? 
Can a friendly robot assistant reduce students’ stress before visiting a professor or an unfamiliar room? 
How accurately can the robot interpret natural language requests about professors, offices, and room numbers? 
How can the robot navigate the CSE second floor while avoiding people and obstacles in a socially appropriate way? 
How do personality features such as fun facts, voice lines, and small waiting games affect the user’s perception of the robot’s liveliness and friendliness?

**3. Methodology**
**3.1 Technical approach**
3.1.1 Technical Subtask 1: Interaction Sequence (Everyone)
First, before technical development began, the team collaboratively planned out all possible interaction scenarios the robot might encounter, along with the corresponding plans of action for each situation. This planning phase allowed us to understand what needed to be done and how to divide our tasks.

3.1.2 Technical Subtask 2: LLM Query and Speech System (Lead: Pranav)
The LLM query and speech system is responsible for interpreting user speech and classifying it into one of four intents: greeting, query, goodbye, and unknown. I started by mapping out how exactly this interaction should flow from start to finish, which is illustrated in the diagram below. I then broke up the different types of language and communication patterns into separate files, which would all be integrated together in the main script. Next, I researched libraries that could handle the speech and language processing, through Google, python docs, and past knowledge. Additionally, I also had to confirm that the libraries we used were compatible with ROS, Python2, Ubuntu 18.04, and Thinkpad 11e. I then wrote the scripts to handle this overall speech interaction with the robot, ensuring that it worked correctly in isolation first before integrating it into the ROS system as its own node, to connect and interact with other scripts.

3.1.3 Technical Subtask 3: Navigation (Lead: Pragna)
The navigation component is responsible for creating and detecting the AprilTag's location within the CSE Building, and sending the correct output to other files. First, I created a database of all the possible destinations within our wing of the CSE Building, along with its corresponding AprilTag IDs. Then after installing the necessary packages I wrote a script to subscribe to the apriltag_ros package, the camera feed, and the LLM query node, to publish navigation information based on what AprilTag is currently being read. This allows the other scripts and robot components to understand where the robot currently is and how to interact with the user and environment. Whenever an AprilTag ID is read, I publish it to the necessary packages so they can use it to determine the robot’s next course of action.

3.1.4 Technical Subtask 4: Movement and Obstacle Avoidance (Lead: Anika)
The movement and obstacle avoidance component is responsible for taking the AprilTag ID detections and corresponding it to the robot’s physical movements. As the robot travels down a hallway, it continuously receives the current AprilTag ID input, and compares it against the target ID, which is the desired destination. Based on this comparison, the robot executes one of several behaviors: moving forward, or stopping and turning around. These main behaviors were developed by first implementing the basic moment commands: turn left, turn right, stop, turn around. This component also handles obstacles by stopping the robot and waiting until the obstacle is cleared before resuming movement. Throughout this process, the navigation_manager.py script also publishes real-time status updates based on the robots current movements respective to the current AprilTag ID, allowing other components of the robot to coordinate their actions accordingly.

3.1.5 Technical Subtask 5: Voice, Visuals, Audio (Lead: Pragna)
The voice, visuals, and audio component gives Benedict its expressive Benedict Cumberbatch inspired persona. I created a database of phrases, audios, and visual images of Benedict Cumberbatch for each scenario. Using the database for phrases, I generated audio files of Benedict Cumberbatch saying those phrases, and mapped the according phrase to its image and audio clip. To ensure the robot responded appropriately during interaction, the voice, visual, and audio outputs were configured to subscribe to the LLM query system’s output, triggering the correct response based on the user’s speech in real time. These tasks created the robot's personality and helped us anthropomorphize the robot, which allowed the users to find it more approachable, fun, and easier to use.

3.1.6 Technical Subtask 6: User Identification and Interaction (Lead: Faizan)
The user identification and interaction component manages the robot’s ability to detect and engage with nearby uses. It gives the robot the ability to know when a potential user arrives, view the AprilTags, and detect obstacles. Development began with calibrating and troubling the camera’s RGB and depth sensors, followed by setting up the raw image topic for publisher/subscriber use. Then I planned how to reuse existing packages from the ‘turtlebot_ws’ (ie. astra_launch, turtlebot_bringup) to integrate with our project’s workflow and environment. Then I designed the FSM for the interaction initiation, and configured the ‘apriltag_ros’ package for our tags family, and updated the build dependencies across the packages.


**3.2 High level overview of human-centric work**
3.2.1 Methodology (Study Design Document)
Overview: We designed our testing and evaluation to specifically evaluate whether or not our robot makes navigating the CSE building easier for students or any other UCSD-affiliated persons

Participants: We recruited participants through this Recruitment Form which included students from CSE 190/276B, our friends, and peers. We put out this form on multiple class platforms to attract participants for our study. Ultimately, we used four participants to test our robot.

Protocol: The participants will be met by a team member on the second floor of the CSE building. During the testing session team member(s) will spectate on the side to make sure no disastrous accidents occur that may damage the robot or harm the user, but will be detached from the participants’ interaction with the robot as much as possible. This is to also allow the team member to take notes on the interaction. To ensure a standardized testing environment, the setup and cleanup will be done before and after the participant leaves. Additionally, the information provided during the testing session, including pre and post experiment questionnaires, will be delivered by following this script. 

Measures: Participants’ names will be hidden from all analyses & anonymized. Numerical answers from the pre and post questionnaires will be compared statistically and used to determine our conclusions in response to our research question. We will also be taking notes during each testing session to make some additional qualitative observations and conclusions


**5. Discussion and Future Work**
Our research and study with using Bearledict in the CSE building shows that using a friendly and interactive robot to assist users with navigating a building with essential resources yet many winding hallways can notably benefit members of the UCSD campus. Such automated assistants can improve productivity and decrease stress for members of the CSE community specifically, which is a benefit that can not be understated when considering dense courseloads and extracurriculars. Our research also contributes to past research done in the human-guide robot interaction space, by providing more relevant data regarding how we can make robots more comfortable for humans to interact with (i.e. giving them a funny and familiar voice like Benedict Cumberbatch’s) and in what contexts robots can create the most positive impact on human users’ experience. 
Possible extensions to this project include expanding the robot’s scope to be capable of navigating around the entire 2nd floor of the CSE building, having one robot guide on each floor of the building, or expanding this project to other hard-to-navigate buildings on the UCSD campus, such as HSS. To enhance the robot’s capabilities, it would be fascinating to have the robot navigate based on select landmarks or make decisions using spatial reasoning, as opposed to solely depending on April tags like Bearledict does. Integrating an LLM into our speech system may also allow user interactions to be more dynamic and move away from relying on pre-set phrases to communicate. An autonomous robot guide for individuals unfamiliar with campus layouts has great potential in being able to assist with navigating complex areas and can make people’s days a little bit easier.
